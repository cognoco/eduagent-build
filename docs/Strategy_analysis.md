# MentoMate — Path to $3k/Month Net

_Strategy memo · June 2026 · based on RevenueCat 2026 subscription benchmarks + category research_

## The target

$3,000/month net ≈ **270–300 paying subscribers** (≈220–250 if Family/Pro mix is meaningful), fed by **~10,000 free monthly active users**. Net contribution is ~$11–13 per Plus sub; LTV ~$120–170, so any channel with CAC under ~$60 is viable.

## The odds

- **~30–40%** over a 2-year horizon **if** you launch soon and work distribution persistently.
- **Under 10%** if launch keeps sliding while pre-launch analysis deepens.

This is a top-decile outcome: only ~17% of subscription apps ever reach $1k/month within two years, and education is winner-take-all (top 10% of education apps capture 96% of revenue). **The default outcome is failure — every decision should be made knowing that.**

## Why customers pick this over free ChatGPT / Gemini

The existential question. For a disciplined adult who just wants things explained, free ChatGPT is already good enough and improves every quarter (memory, voice, study mode). **The app cannot win as a better chat — it loses to free.** It wins only on two jobs free chatbots structurally don't do:

- **Job 1 — make learning actually stick (gym-membership logic).** ChatGPT answers questions but never *manages* learning: it doesn't track what you studied weeks ago, doesn't schedule review, doesn't summon you before you forget, doesn't return on its own initiative. The spine here is the opposite — curriculum broken into topics, an SRS review queue that calls you at the right moment, mastery that is *verified* (the Challenge Round only marks something mastered when every concept checks out), and a mentor that remembers the whole relationship. People don't pay for information (it's free); they pay for structure and accountability — the same reason they pay for a gym when running outside is free.
- **Job 2 — be trustworthy with a teenager (parent logic).** A parent's relationship with ChatGPT is adversarial — it's how the kid avoids learning by getting homework done in 30 seconds. Free chatbots optimise for satisfying the user right now; a mentor must sometimes refuse to hand over the answer. Parents aren't buying a chatbot for their child — they're buying oversight: progress tied to verified mastery (not the kid's self-report), family structure, fail-closed minor guardrails, and a vendor posture chosen for under-18 privacy. Gemini contractually won't serve under-18-directed use at all.

**The honest caveat:** the moat is real but narrow and eroding — OpenAI is moving toward study modes and parental controls, closing the gap from their side. The defensible window is to be visibly better at these two jobs **and make the difference felt in the first session**, because a trial user compares you to ChatGPT within minutes. The customers you keep are buying discipline and proof; the ones who churn back to free were always buying answers — don't design or advertise for them. Practical consequence: every piece of marketing sells the outcome and the system ("she still remembers it a month later," "you see exactly what he's mastered"), never the chat. Advertising "an AI you can talk to about any subject" is advertising ChatGPT, for money.

## What decides it (in order)

1. **Distribution is the binding constraint.** It is currently unsolved, and zero market evidence (retention, conversion, channel) exists yet. The product risk is largely retired; the market risk is almost entirely intact. Nothing else you build matters until D7/D30 retention from real strangers exists.
2. **Churn, not installs, is the game.** Only 14–17% of monthly subscribers survive 12 months; ~40% don't survive their first renewal. Holding 300 subs means acquiring ~250 new payers/year just to stand still. AI apps churn ~30% faster (novelty-heavy cohort — plan as if it applies).
3. **The funnel.** At the 2.2% education install-to-paid median, 300 subs needs ~13,600 installs — before replacing churn.

## Strategy (the four moves that follow from the evidence)

1. **Annual-first pricing.** Annual retains 44% at 12 months vs. 17% monthly (2.6×). The reverse trial should land on an annual offer, monthly as fallback. Highest-confidence lever in the whole analysis.
2. **Sell against private tutoring, never against apps.** At €18.99 (≈2× the $9.99 category median) you lose every app-store price comparison but win most comparisons against human tutoring. Private tutoring is a large, established spend in most markets, and a meaningful share is bought for capable kids to get ahead — advancement, not just rescue. Frame the price against an hour of tutoring, not against other apps.
3. **Organic is the model; paid is a measured experiment.** At median performance paid ads cost $65–320 per sub while the median ed app earns ~$0.40/install in 60 days — ads would eat half to two-thirds of the profit target. ASO, word-of-mouth, content, and your own network for seeding are the engine. If you test paid at all, do it in lower-CPI markets and budget it as learning, not growth.
4. **Instrument analytics before spending a euro on acquisition.** You cannot run this funnel blind, and retention is the one number no benchmark can answer for you.

## Genuine advantages (durable)

- EU minors-compliance moat — rare and defensible; most AI wrappers can't legally serve under-18s in the EU.
- The two-jobs differentiation above (accountability + parent trust) — the part free chatbots structurally don't do.
- Top-decile engineering quality and EU/multilingual focus.

## Open question

- **Launch market is not yet chosen.** The benchmarks above are category-level and market-agnostic; pick the launch market on your own grounds (language coverage, your network, regulatory fit), then localize the funnel and pricing-vs-tutoring frame to it.

## Bottom line

A top-decile product on a bottom-quartile go-to-market position. The only viable sell is the two jobs chatbots don't do — accountability that makes learning stick, and parent-trusted oversight with verified progress. Roughly a 1-in-3 shot over two years if you ship the 13+ launch soon and work distribution hard — under 1-in-10 if launch keeps sliding. **Launch is the single highest-priority move.**
