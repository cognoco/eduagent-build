## Amendment (2026-06-15, architect) — ADR provenance discipline: reconstruct vs. launder, L3-in-passing-only, operator-signed ratification, dedicated change-sets

**Why this is recorded.** The model already says, in three scattered places, that an ADR's authority must not flow *backwards* from an ephemeral plan: the §I.2 north-star ("legacy canon we cannot trace back is grandfathered, **not reverse-engineered into invented ADRs**"), the Part III reconstruction sanction (the structured `ARCH-N` drain, stamped `reconstructed`), and the Risks stamp rule ("after-the-fact ADRs are stamped `reconstructed YYYY-MM-DD` … rather than invented"). A 2026-06-14/15 provenance audit (WP `WI-752`) found the arrow reversed in practice: several ADRs were reverse-engineered **from** feature plans and ratified as canon without architectural vetting — born inside `feat()` PRs, carrying self-asserted "Deciders: PM (owner)" / "+ Codex" sign-off, with canon prose laced with plan-phase labels (`S0`/`S4`) as load-bearing actors. The principle was *implied* but never stated as an enforceable authoring rule. This amendment states it.

**1. Reconstruct vs. launder — the bright line.** Both motions produce an ADR from a non-ADR artifact, but they run in opposite directions, and only one is sanctioned:

- **Reconstruction (sanctioned).** Recording a decision *already made and (usually) built*, recovered from a legacy artifact — an `ARCH-N` register entry, a shipped plan's "key design decisions", or the code itself. The decision **predates** the ADR; the ADR documents reality. Per Part III + the Risks stamp rule it is recorded plainly, stamped `reconstructed YYYY-MM-DD`, and where the *why* is unrecoverable it is stated as such rather than invented. `MMT-ADR-0006` (`ARCH-14`) and `MMT-ADR-0018` (`ARCH-8`) are the worked examples.
- **Laundering (banned).** Minting an ADR to **retroactively bless a NEW choice** that a feature plan or PR introduced — the plan is the choice's actual origin and spine, and the ADR is an after-the-fact wrapper conferring canon-authority the choice never earned through architectural vetting. This inverts the layer model (L3 → L2, instead of L2 → L1, which L3 *implements*) and is prohibited. A new choice surfaced by feature work is welcome — but it must be **vetted as architecture and ratified on its own merits** (rules 2–4), never back-filled from the plan.

The separating test: **did the decision exist, made and usually built, before the ADR? → reconstruct (stamp it). Is the ADR the first place the choice is argued or blessed, originating in an L3 plan? → that argument must happen as architecture, not as plan-laundering.**

**2. An ADR's spine is the decision itself; L3 is cited only in passing.** The body of an ADR — Context, Decision, Alternatives — must stand on architectural reasoning, not on an ephemeral document's authority. An L3 doc (`docs/specs/`, `docs/plans/`, `docs/runbooks/`) may be referenced **only in passing** — a pointer to where the feature work lives, or where a column-level detail is owned — **never as the decision's spine or support.** Two concrete prohibitions:
- An ADR may not cite a plan in a way that makes the plan the *reason the decision is what it is*.
- **Canon (L1) carries no plan-as-authority reference and no feature-phase label (`S0`–`S6` and the like) as a load-bearing actor** — phases are rollout sequencing (L3), not architecture. Naming a phase *in passing* to locate work is tolerable; a phase used as the subject that *owns* a behaviour ("the S4 phase repoints the ledger") is not.

**3. Ratification is operator-signed; proposing ≠ ratifying.** Anyone — any agent, any contributor — may **draft** an ADR at `Status: Proposed`. An ADR reaches `Status: Accepted` **only by the architect/operator's actual sign-off act.** The anti-pattern this bans is an agent *asserting* the operator's authority — writing "Deciders: PM (owner)" (or naming "+ Codex" / "+ Claude" as the ratifier) **without the operator having signed.** The Deciders line of an Accepted ADR must record a real architect sign-off, not a claimed one. This is the §II.1 gate's missing companion: §II.1 says *what* earns an ADR; this says *who* may move it to Accepted. (Reconstructions under rule 1 still require operator sign-off to reach Accepted; the `reconstructed` stamp records provenance, not ratification.)

**4. ADRs land in dedicated change-sets, not feature PRs.** A new or amended ADR is committed in its own `docs(adr)` change-set — lockstep with the exact canon lines it changes (§II.2) — **not** born inside a `feat()` feature PR. Rationale: an ADR ratified inside the PR that implements it receives the *implementation's* review, not *architectural* review; that structural co-location is what lets laundering pass unseen.
- **Calibration (so the rule does not cry wolf).** The disqualifying signature is the **conjunction** — non-architect author **and** feature-PR birth **and** self-asserted non-architect ratification (often **and** plan-spine-anchoring) — **not** the bare commit verb. An architect-authored ADR that happened to ride a `feat()` commit (e.g. `MMT-ADR-0020`; or the `feat(docs)` ADR-layer bootstrap that installed `0000`–`0006`) is **not** a launder — it carries real sign-off. Forward, prefer the dedicated `docs(adr)` change-set regardless; the conjunction is what any guard must key on.

**5. Forward guard.** [OPERATOR SUB-DECISION — see the recommendation below; this paragraph is written for the recommended option and is struck/replaced if you choose otherwise.] Going forward the §II.5 ratchet is extended with an **ADR-provenance check** (forward-only, baseline-grandfathered like every other ratchet), implemented as a tracked child of `WI-752`. It mechanically enforces the two checkable failure modes from the audit — (i) a commit that **adds** a `docs/adr/MMT-ADR-*.md` file under a `feat(...)` subject is blocked with a message pointing here (overridable for the rare architect feat-commit via an explicit allow-comment + baseline entry), and (ii) a new ADR at `Status: Accepted` whose Deciders line carries **no architect token** fails the lint. The semantic plan-spine-anchor check (rule 2) stays a human/review concern — it is not cheaply mechanizable and a fuzzy guard would erode trust.
