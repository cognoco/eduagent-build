## Session summary

### B1 — The real age floor
**Question.** B1 — The real age floor. Product intent: a child of ANY age can be a supervised learner as long as a parent verifiably approves (VPC — verifiable parental consent, a recognised method to confirm the approver is really the parent, not a tick-box). Today the code hard-codes an "11" floor with NO documented legal rationale. The spike read: with VPC, any-age is lawful, so the floor is a PRODUCT / APP-STORE-RATING decision, not a legal hard stop. QUESTION: confirm or correct — is there any legal floor below which a consented child simply cannot be a user, given VPC, anywhere in the EU, US, or UK? This decides whether we are free to set the floor ourselves. Keep crisp the distinction between (i) a usage floor (may the child be a user at all), (ii) design/protection duties that scale with age but do not bar usage, and (iii) a capacity-to-self-contract/pay floor (that belongs to B3a).

#### 1) Counsel draft
- **Capture:** Rule + basis (with one embedded Parameter: the strictest VPC trigger is COPPA's under-13 line, and one Monitor flag on the EU/UK app-store age-rating overlay).  ·  **Confidence:** high
- **Binary read:** No legal floor. Given VPC, no EU/US/UK instrument sets a minimum age below which a consented, supervised child cannot be a user; the floor is yours to set on product/rating grounds.
- **Answer:** No legal floor exists below which a verifiably-consented child cannot be a user, anywhere in EU/US/UK. With genuine VPC (COPPA-grade verification of the holder of parental responsibility), a child of any age — including an infant operated by a parent — may lawfully be a supervised user; none of GDPR Art 8, COPPA, or the UK Children's Code sets a minimum age below which use is barred. The hard-coded "11" floor therefore has no legal mandate and is a product / app-store-rating decision you are free to set yourselves. The legal regimes impose only (ii) protection/design duties that intensify as age drops (Children's Code high-privacy defaults, GDPR Art 25, AI Act age-vulnerability and emotion-inference bars) and (iii) a separate self-contract/pay-capacity floor — but that belongs to B3a and does not bar usage; the paying adult, not the child, is the contracting party.
- **Reasoning:** Strictest-of-three chain. (1) US/COPPA is the most demanding on mechanism: it requires VPC for any online collection from a child under 13, and the 2025 rule tightens it (per-purpose, never-integral separate consent). But COPPA is structured as a consent/notice/retention regime keyed to a TRIGGER age (13), not a minimum age of use — §312.5 conditions collection on VPC; §312.10 scales retention/deletion duties. Nowhere does COPPA say a child below some age cannot be a user. So once VPC is genuine, an under-13 (any age) is lawful. (2) EU/GDPR Art 8 likewise sets a digital-consent age (13-16, MS-set; you ship the strictest 16) and a parental-verification duty under 8(2); below the line the parent consents. Art 25 + Recitals 38/71 add by-default protection that scales with youth. None bars usage. (3) UK Children's Code applies to all under-18 as a design ceiling — its 15 standards raise duties (high-privacy defaults, best-interests, age-appropriate experience) but set no minimum age. Scotland s.2(4A) even contemplates competence at 12, confirming young children are recognised data subjects/users, not excluded persons. The AI Act layer is age-invariant (Art 50 transparency, Annex III high-risk, Art 5(1)(f) emotion-inference bar, 5(1)(b) age-vulnerability) — it governs tutor behaviour, not eligibility. The three categories in the question therefore resolve cleanly: (i) usage floor = none in privacy law given VPC; (ii) protection/design duties = real and intensifying as age drops (this is where a younger floor would have to earn its keep operationally, e.g. age-appropriate copy, stricter minimisation, no behavioural tracking — DPDPA/AADC); (iii) capacity-to-self-contract/pay = a distinct floor that lives in B3a (the child is not the contracting party; the paying adult is), and store-delegated payment means the store adjudicates capacity. The only genuine external age constraint is the app-store rating/Declared-Age-Range/App-Store-Accountability-Act layer — a product/commercial regime, not a privacy-law usage floor. Conclusion: the '11' floor is yours to set; pick it on product, safeguarding-capacity, and store-rating grounds, and document THAT rationale (its current absence of a documented basis is the real defect to cure).
- **Regime divergence:** EU: consent age is a 13-16 band (you ship 16); Art 8(2) verification is "reasonable efforts" proportionate to risk/tech, NOT COPPA-grade VPC, and below the line the parent consents — no usage floor. US: COPPA's under-13 line is a VPC TRIGGER with the most prescriptive verification mechanics (and 2025 per-purpose/never-integral separate consent), but still no minimum age of use; 13-15 hands certain rights to the teen (CCPA/CPRA §1798.120(c)). Net: the three diverge on (a) the consent-age number, (b) the verification standard (COPPA VPC strictest), and (c) the breadth of protective design duties (UK AADC reaches highest, to 18) — but converge on the dispositive point: none sets a floor below which a verifiably-consented supervised child cannot be a user.

That election forecloses the lighter non-disclosure methods (email-plus 312.5(b)(2)(viii) AND text-message-plus (ix)) and requires a DISCLOSURE-GRADE enumerated method: signed consent form returned (i), monetary-transaction-with-notice (ii), toll-free phone (iii), video-conference (iv), gov-ID match (v), knowledge-based authentication (vi), or face-match-to-ID (vii). (2) COPPA is STRICTER on architecture than a single consent: the 2025 rule [312.5(a)(2)] requires a SEPARATE, unbundled, per-purpose VPC for the third-party-disclosure purpose, wrapped in a 312.4 direct notice that names the LLM-vendor disclosure, and re-verifiable with the same rigor at 312.6 withdrawal/deletion. (3) EU/UK reach a COMPATIBLE — not identical-source — destination: GDPR Art 8(2) and UK GDPR Art 8 require only proportionate 'reasonable efforts...taking into consideration available technology' [EDPB Guidelines 05/2020 §7.1; EDPB Statement 1/2025; AADC Std 3]; the same disclosure-grade method SATISFIES that proportionality for a high-risk AI + child context and exceeds the floor, but neither EU nor UK independently MANDATES the COPPA menu. NET, build to: a COPPA disclosure-grade enumerated VPC method, obtained as a separate per-purpose consent, inside a purpose-naming notice, re-verifiable at withdrawal, with tokenised non-retained evidence. The method-strength LEVEL is a Rule (no self-declaration, no email-plus, disclosure-grade only); WHICH enumerated method and which vendor is a Parameter deferred to procurement, gated on this answer.

### B2b — Boundary-crossing birth-year verification
**Question.** B2b — Boundary-crossing verification. Birth-year is correctable in-app. QUESTION: if someone EDITS their birth year in a way that CROSSES the consent line — flipping themselves from "needs a parent" to "does not" — what VERIFICATION STANDARD must we apply so a child cannot simply TYPE their way past the gate, while a genuine adult who mistyped can still fix it? Address the asymmetry: a crossing-up edit (into less protection) must be verified; an honest non-crossing correction may just save. Cover crossing the under-13 COPPA line vs crossing a 13–16 GDPR line.

#### 1) Counsel draft
- **Capture:** Rule + Parameter  ·  **Confidence:** high
- **Binary read:** n/a — verification standard. The operative rule: self-declaration is the floor for a non-crossing edit; a crossing-up edit (into less protection) requires verification proportionate to the line crossed, and the more-protective state persists until that verification clears.
- **Answer:** Build an asymmetric, direction-aware age-edit gate. A non-crossing correction (e.g. 14→15, both inside the same protection band) may save on self-declaration. An edit that CROSSES a consent line in the direction of LESS protection — flipping the person from "needs a parent" to "does not" — must NOT take effect on the typed value alone; the typed value only triggers verification proportionate to the line being crossed. For the COPPA under-13 line (US, strictest), a crossing-up edit unlocks NOTHING until you apply the same gate you would apply to a fresh adult signup PLUS treat the prior state as a child: the lower-protection state cannot activate the AI-tutor disclosure until age is established by a means that a child cannot self-satisfy (the COPPA "reasonable effort" / age-assurance standard, not a tick-box). For a 13–16 GDPR/UK consent line, the crossing-up edit requires age assurance proportionate to the risk (EDPB Statement 1/2025) — not full VPC of a parent, but more than self-declaration: a method "reasonably likely" to detect a child typing past the gate. In all cases the safe default while verification is pending is to REMAIN in the more-protective state (browse-only preview, no AI call) — never grant the lower-protection capability optimistically and claw back. Honest downward/non-crossing edits (into MORE protection, or within a band) save freely; this is fail-safe and never a circumvention risk.
- **Reasoning:** The legal chain runs from the load-bearing fact: the AI-tutor turn discloses the learner's words to a third-party LLM, which is the COPPA §312.5 / GDPR Art 8 trigger. A birth-year edit that crosses a consent line UPWARD is an attempt to remove that trigger. Strictest-of-three: COPPA controls the under-13 line. A self-typed age is 'collection' (§312.2) but is NOT one of the §312.5(b) methods 'reasonably calculated' to establish the relevant fact; and once the operator holds data that the user WAS under 13, it has actual knowledge that a bare self-edit does not dispel. So the crossing-up edit must NOT auto-unlock disclosure — it must invoke an age-assurance step the child cannot self-satisfy, and until that clears the user stays in the no-disclosure browse-only state (which is itself lawful precisely because it collects nothing). The GDPR/UK 13–16 line is governed by Art 8(2)'s 'reasonable efforts … taking into consideration available technology' — proportionate, not VPC-grade, but above self-declaration; EDPB Statement 1/2025 sets assurance proportionate to risk, and Art 5(1)(c)/Art 25 force the least-intrusive method plus a high-privacy default while pending. The asymmetry is legally principled, not just UX: a crossing-up edit sheds protection and so bears the verification burden; a non-crossing or downward edit ADDS or preserves protection and is fail-safe, so self-declaration suffices (forcing verification there would breach minimisation by collecting more than necessary). Practical build: classify every edit by (old band, new band, direction); only an up-crossing into less protection gates; the gate's strength keys to which line is crossed (under-13 = strongest proportionate method ≈ fresh-signup VPC posture; 13–16 = proportionate step-up); the more-protective state is the default until the gate clears, never optimistic grant + clawback. An honest adult who mistyped themselves as a child made a DOWN-crossing edit originally (into more protection) and corrects UP — they hit the gate, but for an adult any compliant assurance method resolves it in one step, so the genuine-adult path is preserved without letting a child type through.
- **Regime divergence:** US (COPPA): the hardest line is under-13. A crossing edit out of under-13 must clear an age step a child cannot self-satisfy, and prior under-13 data is 'actual knowledge' that a self-edit does not erase — so the gate here is effectively fresh-signup-grade. COPPA imposes no verification at the 13+ self-edits (it stops at 13). EU (GDPR Art 8): the consent line is Member-State-set 13–16; a crossing-up edit triggers Art 8(2) 'reasonable efforts … available technology' — proportionate assurance, lighter than COPPA VPC, but above self-declaration, with Art 25 high-privacy default pending. UK: consent age fixed at 13 (DPA 2018 s.9), so the GDPR-style verification attaches at the 13 line; ON TOP, the AADC (Standard 3) and the OSA 'highly effective age assurance' regime push the up-crossing toward a stronger-than-self-declaration method even for the 13-band capability, making the UK the strictest for the 13-line specifically. Net strictest-of-three to build: under-13 up-crossing = fresh-VPC-grade gate (COPPA); 13–16 up-crossing = proportionate 'reasonably/highly effective' assurance, not self-declaration (UK AADC/OSA + GDPR Art 8). Non-crossing/downward edits = self-declaration everywhere.
- **Bases:**

- **Answer:** Ingesting a platform-supplied age signal (Apple/Google Declared Age Range API) and using it on a stricter-wins basis against the user's self-declaration (D4) is LAWFUL across EU/US/UK, and is in fact the privacy-preferred path because it lets you set the high-privacy default without collecting hard identifiers. But the signal is INPUT, not adjudication: it carries its own duties — (1) transparency: tell the user/parent you receive and use a platform age signal and to what effect (it can only tighten, never loosen, protections), in the notice at or before ingestion; (2) accuracy/data-minimisation: store only the coarse band you act on (not a precise age or raw identifier), date-stamp it, and never silently override a stricter self-declared/known age; (3) the hard ceiling — a platform age signal is NEVER a substitute for verifiable parental consent (COPPA VPC) or for GDPR Art 8(2) parental-authority verification. It can route a user into the consent-gated path; it cannot itself authorise the third-party LLM disclosure. Treat the App Store Accountability regime as a Monitor: it is new (US state laws live 1 Jan 2026), the API shape and any reliance "safe harbour" are unsettled, and over-reliance is itself a compliance risk.
- **Reasoning:** Chain, strictest-of-three: (1) Lawfulness of ingestion. Reading a coarse age band the platform exposes and using it to decide whether to route a user into the consent-gated (no-AI) path is processing for a child-protection/compliance purpose. Under GDPR Art 25 and AADC Std 3, establishing age with risk-appropriate certainty to apply a high-privacy default is exactly what regulators want; EDPB Statement 1/2025 blesses proportionate, data-minimising age assurance. So ingestion is not merely lawful — it is the privacy-preferred route versus collecting ID. (2) Stricter-wins (D4) is the only lawful reconciliation direction. The signal may tighten (push to browse-only / require consent) but must never loosen a stricter self-declared or known age, because a looser-but-wrong signal that lowered a child's protection would breach Art 5(1)(d) accuracy and the AADC best-interests/high-privacy-default duties. (3) Duties that attach to using it. Notice (Arts 12–14): receiving an external age signal is processing the user/parent must be told about, in plain terms, at or before ingestion — what you receive, from whom, and that it can only increase protection. Accuracy + minimisation (Art 5(1)(c)/(d)): store only the band you act on, date-stamp it, do not retain a precise age or raw platform identifier you do not need. (4) The hard ceiling — not a VPC substitute. This is the load-bearing point. COPPA §312.5(b) enumerates VPC methods; a platform age band is not one. GDPR Art 8(2) requires reasonable efforts to verify the holder of parental responsibility; a declared-age API does not verify the parent. So the signal can decide which gate a user hits, but the third-party LLM disclosure (the COPPA/GDPR trigger) still requires real VPC / Art 8(2) verification before the AI tutor runs. Net strictest line: ingest and rely stricter-wins, give notice, minimise, and treat the signal as routing-only — VPC/Art 8(2) verification is independently required for the AI disclosure.
