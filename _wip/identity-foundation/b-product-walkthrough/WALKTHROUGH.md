# B-product Walkthrough — the script  *(read `FACILITATOR-BRIEF.md` first)*

Six segments. Each runs the loop: **teach → check-in → decide → play back → capture.** **[Bracketed italics]**
are stage directions to you, the facilitator — not spoken. Plain about the *words*; never plain about the
*substance*. Anchor to how the product works today; name a model term only after its job.

**Order & what each decides:**
1. The owner unbundles → *how a multi-hat parent sees the app*
2. Who it's for → *audience statement; "homework helper" framing*
3. Age & consent → *default sign-in; browse-only preview; teen self-pay*
4. Two kinds of grown-up oversight → *what happens when the last parent leaves; visibility when a child grows up*
5. People becoming a family → *joining up; a child asking a parent in; consent-reminder limits*
6. Changing over time → *the "growing up" moment; the "moved country" moment; fixing a wrong birth year*

---

## Segment 1 — The owner unbundles into separate jobs  → decision: the multi-hat parent's home

### Beat 1 — Teach · ~2 min
> "One change underpins almost every decision today, so let's ground it first — in terms of how the product
> *works*, not how it's stored.
>
> Right now, one 'account' is really three things wearing the same coat: it's the **login**, it's the
> **family**, and it's the **person**. And whoever 'owns' that account does three more things at once — they
> **run it**, they **pay** for it, and they're the **parent**. One thing, all bundled.
>
> The foundation unbundles that. **[open concept-map.html]** The **person** becomes their own thing, and they
> own their learning for good. Their **login** is separate and optional — no login just means someone set things
> up for them, like a young child on a parent's phone. The **family** is just a thin group: it never owns the
> person. And 'the owner' splits into three separate jobs: **running the group**, **paying** (which now gives
> access to *nobody's* learning), and **being the parent** (the consent relationship to one specific child).
> 'Being allowed to see a learner's work' is its own explicit link too — never something the group hands out
> automatically."

### Beat 2 — Check-in (not a test) · ~1 min
> "So the whole move is: the single 'owner' becomes three separate jobs — run it, pay, be the parent — and they
> don't have to come as a package any more. That's the shift the rest of today builds on. Does that match how
> you've pictured it, or does any part feel off?"

**[Conversational — invite her to react or correct, then move on. If she pushes back on the split, follow it:
that's signal, possibly a ripple. Don't interrogate.]**

### Beat 3 — Decide · ~5–8 min
> "Here's the question this unlocks. Picture a parent running a family: she **runs** the group, she's the
> **payer**, she's the **parent** of two kids — and she might be **learning** herself. In the old world that
> was all just 'the owner,' one screen. Now those are separate. So when she opens the app, does she see **one
> combined home** that blends all of it, or **separate spaces** she moves between (a 'managing the family'
> space vs. 'my own learning')?"

**[Neutral options — A: one blended home; B: separate spaces. No recommendation. Grill:]**
- *If blended:* "What's at the top — her own next lesson, or the kids' progress? When they compete, who wins?"
- *If separate:* "How does she switch? And does a parent who's *also* a serious learner feel second-class in
  either space?"
- *Force the edge:* "Now a parent who runs the family, pays, and is the parent — but isn't learning herself.
  Does your answer still hold, or does the no-learning-herself case want something different?"

### Beat 4 — Play back
> "So: **[restate in her words]** — e.g. 'separate spaces, default to family-management, a clear switch to my
> own learning; the parent who isn't learning lands straight in family-management with no empty "my learning"
> shell.' Right?"

### Beat 5 — Capture + ripple
**[Ledger:]** `E6 — multi-role surface. [P✓ <date>]` — *<her decision>.* Re-anchor: presentation over the
person's several jobs (run / pay / parent / learner); the structure supports either, so **no structural change.**
**[Ripple check:** did she ask for a *new* power (e.g. "a parent who can edit the child's lessons")? → stop,
flag the architect. Pure on-screen arrangement → no ripple.]**

## Segment 2 — Who it's for  → decisions: audience statement + "homework helper"

### Beat 1 — Teach · ~1.5 min
> "Because the **person** is now the center — not a school, not a parent's account — the product travels with
> the learner. A nine-year-old, a nineteen-year-old, a forty-five-year-old: structurally they're the same kind
> of thing, a person who owns their learning. So 'any age' isn't a tagline we bolted on; it's just what the
> shape already is."

### Beat 2 — Check-in
> "Does that line up with how you think about the audience — the learner at the center, at any age?"

### Beat 3 — Decide
**Audience statement:**
> "How do we *say* who this is for? The honest description is 'a serious learner of any age.' Do we state that
> plainly as the audience — or narrow it? And who's the *primary* learner we'd optimise the experience for, even
> if the door is wider?"

**"Homework helper":**
> "'Homework helper' — is that how we *describe* the product, or just one way in the door? If it's the headline,
> does it quietly cap us to school-age homework and box out the older serious learner? Or is it a friendly
> entrance into the broader thing?"

**[Grill:** chase the consequence of each — "if 'homework helper' is the headline, what does the 19-year-old
think when they land? If 'serious learners of any age' is the headline, does the parent of a 10-year-old still
feel it's for them?"]**

### Beat 4 — Play back
> "So: **[restate]** — e.g. 'audience = serious learners of any age, primary focus <X>; "homework helper" is a
> way-in / campaign frame, not the product's ceiling.'"

### Beat 5 — Capture
**[Ledger:]** `C3 — audience statement [P✓]` and `C2 — homework-helper framing [P✓]` — *<decisions>.* Both are
positioning calls; **no structural change.**

## Segment 3 — Age & consent: what a child can do alone  → decisions: default sign-in, browse-only preview, teen self-pay

### Beat 1 — Teach · ~2.5 min
> "Whether a child can just start on their own or needs a parent to say yes first depends on **age and country**
> — there's no single 'under 18' line; the age at which a child can consent for themselves runs roughly 13 to
> 16 depending on the country. Below that, a parent has to approve.
>
> And there's one hard rule we don't get to set: **before a parent has approved, a young child can't use the AI
> tutor at all.** Using it sends the child's words out to an outside AI service — and the law (COPPA in the US,
> GDPR in Europe) treats sending a child's information to an outside service as something a parent must consent
> to first. So 'no AI before a parent says yes' isn't our policy choice; it's the legal floor."

### Beat 2 — Check-in
> "So two things move with age and country: whether the child needs a parent's yes, and — until that yes —
> whether they can touch the AI at all. Comfortable with that as the backdrop?"

### Beat 3 — Decide
**Browse-only preview:**
> "While a child waits for their parent to approve, do we show them *anything*? We could offer a **browse-only
> look** — they can see what's there, but no AI and nothing collected — or just a plain 'waiting for your
> grown-up' screen. The browse-only option is only allowed *because* it doesn't use the AI or collect anything.
> Is the preview worth building, or is a clean waiting screen enough?"

**Default sign-in for a teen:**
> "When a teen signs up, do we default them to **their own private login**, or to being **set up under a
> parent**? Both are allowed — this is just which we reach for first. Where does your instinct sit, and does it
> change by age within 'teen'?"

**Teen self-pay:**
> "A teen old enough to pay through their own app-store account *can*, technically, subscribe themselves — the
> app store handles whether they're allowed to pay. Do we **offer** self-pay to under-18s in the product, or
> always route paying through an adult? (Paying gives no access to anyone's learning either way — this is purely
> 'do we surface it.')"

**[Grill each to a concrete case: a 14-year-old in Germany; a 16-year-old in Norway with their own card.]**

### Beat 4 — Play back · Beat 5 — Capture
**[Ledger:]** `D2 — preview [P✓]`, `D1 — default sign-in [P✓]`, `E0-exposure — teen self-pay [P✓]` — *<decisions>.*
Re-anchor D2: must remain no-AI / no-collection to stay lawful pre-consent. **No structural change** (all are
defaults/surfacing within the locked model). **[Ripple check** on D1: if she wants a default the model forbids —
e.g. a young child with their own login *and* no parent — flag the architect.]**

## Segment 4 — Two kinds of grown-up oversight  → decisions: last parent leaves; visibility when a child grows up

### Beat 1 — Teach · ~2 min
> "There are two different ways a grown-up relates to a child's learning, and keeping them apart matters.
> **[refer to the two links on concept-map.html]** One is the **consent-giver** — the parent who says 'yes, my
> child may use this,' and who can take that back. The other is a **helper who can see** — like a tutor who's
> allowed to look at *one* child's progress to help them. They're separate: a tutor isn't a parent, and a
> parent's permission for the child to *be here* doesn't by itself let anyone *see* the child's work. Seeing is
> always its own explicit allowance, for one named person."

### Beat 2 — Check-in
> "So 'who may say yes for the child' and 'who may see the child's work' are two different switches, not one.
> Does that match your mental model?"

### Beat 3 — Decide
**Last parent leaves (ripple-prone):**
> "Picture a child whose only consenting parent leaves or deletes their account. **[open scenarios.html →
> last-guardian]** The structure keeps the child and all their learning safe — nothing is deleted. But
> product-wise: do we hold the child in a safe 'needs a grown-up' state and let **another adult be attached** —
> and if so, *who* can step in, and how do we make sure it's legitimate? Or something else?"

**[RIPPLE-PRONE — if she introduces a custody arrangement or a 'transfer to another parent' the model hasn't
accounted for, STOP and put it to the architect.]**

**Visibility when a child grows up (the opt-in tail):**
> "When a child grows past the consent age, the parent stops automatically seeing their work — the now-older
> teen controls who sees. How should that *feel*? Does the parent get told it's switching off? Can they ask the
> teen to keep access — with the teen deciding?"

### Beat 4 — Play back · Beat 5 — Capture
**[Ledger:]** `E5 — last-guardian [P✓ or flagged]`, `E1-visibility-tail [P✓]` — *<decisions>.* Re-anchor: the
'needs a grown-up' state is a named holding state; attaching a new adult is the guardian-attachment path
(architect confirms it's covered). **Log any ripple.**

## Segment 5 — People becoming a family  → decisions: joining up; child asks a parent in; reminder limits

### Beat 1 — Teach · ~2 min
> "So far we've pictured a parent setting up a child. But people also arrive **separately** — a teen signs up
> alone, a parent already has an account — and then they want to connect. **[open scenarios.html → join]** The
> shape lets two separate people link into one family. One safety rule is already locked, though: **a child can
> never pick their own consent-giver.** A child may *ask* a parent to join — but the grown-up is the one who
> accepts and proves they're really the parent. Never the reverse."

### Beat 2 — Check-in
> "Make sense that 'a child asks, a verified adult accepts' is the only safe direction — never 'a child appoints
> their own parent'?"

### Beat 3 — Decide
**Joining up (consolidation):**
> "A teen signed up alone; their parent already runs a family account. They want to become one household. From
> the teen's side it feels like 'join my family'; underneath, the teen moves into the parent's family and their
> solo account is retired. Does that match your intent? What does the **teen initiate** vs the **parent
> approve**? (One nuance: if the teen still needs a parent's consent, the parent becomes the consent-giver too;
> if the teen's old enough to decide for themselves, the parent is just the payer / a helper — not automatically
> allowed to see the work.)"

**Child asks a parent in:**
> "How does that 'ask a parent to join' actually look — and how does the parent **prove they're the parent**?
> (The proof part is a question we'll take to the lawyer; right now I just want the shape of the flow.)"

**Reminder limits:**
> "When a child sends the request to a parent and nothing happens — how long before they can resend? How many
> times? Any cooldown so we don't spam the parent? Pick numbers, or say 'reasonable defaults.'"

### Beat 4 — Play back · Beat 5 — Capture
**[Ledger:]** `E12 — consolidation [P✓]`, `E13 — child-asks-parent [P✓]`, `D3 — reminder limits [P✓]` —
*<decisions>.* Re-anchor: consolidation = move the person's home to the parent's family + retire the solo group;
the parent's resulting link depends on whether the teen still needs consent. **Flag the parent-proof question to
counsel; flag any ripple to the architect.**

## Segment 6 — Changing over time, without losing anything  → decisions: growing up; moved country; fix birth year

### Beat 1 — Teach · ~1.5 min
> "Two things change on their own. A child **grows past the consent age** — old enough to decide for themselves.
> And a person **moves to a different country**, where the rules differ. The system notices these by itself —
> even for someone who hasn't opened the app in months — and re-checks what's needed. Through all of it, nobody
> loses their learning."

### Beat 2 — Check-in
> "Comfortable that 'we re-check on a birthday or a move, automatically' is the behaviour — not 'only when they
> happen to log in'?"

### Beat 3 — Decide
**Growing up:**
> "The day a child becomes old enough to run their own account — do we **automatically hand them the reins**, or
> **prompt them to take over** and leave things as they are until they do? (One part is fixed: the parent's
> ability to *see* their work switches off until the now-older teen chooses to allow it again.)"

**Moved to a stricter country:**
> "If someone moves somewhere stricter and now needs a parent's consent they didn't need before, the safe
> behaviour is to **pause the AI** until that's sorted. While paused, what do they see — fully locked, or the
> browse-only look again? And how hard do we push to get it resolved?"

**Wrong birth year:**
> "If someone entered the wrong birth year — accidentally made themselves too young — they must be able to **fix
> it in the app**, never hit a dead-end. How should that correction feel, and what stops someone gaming it?"

### Beat 4 — Play back · Beat 5 — Capture
**[Ledger:]** `E1 — grow-up takeover [P✓]`, `E2 — moved-country feel [P✓]`, `F1-BT-b — birth-year fix [P✓]` —
*<decisions>.* Re-anchor: takeover is the per-dimension crossing (see-switches-off is fixed; account-takeover is
the choosable part); pause = the holding state; birth-year fix = the no-dead-end correction path. **No structural
change** (these are experiences within locked behaviour). **Flag any ripple.**
